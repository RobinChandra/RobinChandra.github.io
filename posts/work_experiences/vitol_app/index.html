Applying for Vitol

Today, I signed my first employment contract offer. It was quite an emotional moment for me, whose path to get a decent job was rough and full of challenges but more on that in the upcoming post. For now, I will discuss more regarding my job application journey with Vitol.

An Initiative

I noticed the job opening a few weeks back, around the end of June, and immediately thought that this might be it. A Trading Analyst role at Vitol Asia. A job that is, at least for me, exciting and I am familiar with. I knew Vitol back then when I was an intern at Chevron, sending bill-of-lading documents to various stakeholders, and Vitol was one of them.

I applied right away and took the initiative to search for any HR personnel I can contact and talk to regarding my application. Fortunately, the HR Head is on LinkedIn! and he was using premium which allowed me to send a message to him directly.

The following day he reviewed my application and requested me for a short video interview. I was so happy knowing that all was going well and promising. I submitted the videos and then I never hear back from them for the next 3 weeks.

One morning, July 16th 2021, I took the initiative to ask for a follow-up regarding my application, as three weeks is quite a long waiting time for a short video interview outcome. It turned out that they are not interested to hire foreigners anymore as the current Employment Pass (work visa) requires them to pay a foreigner at such a high minimum salary, at 4,500 SGD/month as mandated by the Singapore government since last year.

At this point, I was a bit disappointed to know the fact that it was not me unsuitable for them but the politico-economic condition which hinders me to get the job. Fortunately, was graduated from a local university and have to fulfill my 3-year bond. As you might have known, NUS is a local university and somehow given more flexibility for EP assessment. And the fact that I need to serve my tuition grant bond also helps.

I was quite upset with it and decided to browse the Ministry of Manpower’s website to find any ways I can be employed without having that absurdly high starting salary. It was at that time that I realized we can actually check our eligibility for any work pass by self-assessment.

I filled my credentials and assumed a slightly lower salary (average fresh graduate salary) at the Ministry of Manpower’s Self Assessment Tool and VOILA!!! It said I am eligible for Employment Pass. I passed on this valuable info the HR guy know about this and he quickly responded to proceed with my application.

Interview, Interview, and More Interview

A few days later, the HR guy emailed me for an interview with the manager the following day. I was so happy but at the same time scared. I was not prepared at all and the idea of a technical interview sounds scary as well! Nevertheless, it was something I have to pass. I texted my ex-supervisor at Chevron to ask for some advice and she gladly helped me to point out the key areas I have to emphasize and prepare well. I spent the last remaining hours that day skimming through articles and books to get myself prepared for any technical and logic questions.

Just like a blessing from above, the interviewer was urgently unavailable at the agreed timing and the interview was rescheduled to the next day! That’s it, I thought. That was my last chance to be materially and mentally prepared. One piece of advice from me is that this book: “How to Ace the Brainteaser Interview” by John Kador is a must for everyone preparing for an interview.

Here comes the scary part, the interview. It was 2-hours long. The longest time I have been interviewed in my whole life. However, it turned out to be extremely exciting and educational. The interviewer was extremely open-minded, informational, but also willing to test your resilience under stress.

I was asked the whole package of questions, from self introductory, technical, commercial, and even logical questions. No wonder why it took 2-hours to complete. Nevertheless, I enjoyed the whole experience and I honestly got more relaxed towards the end of the interview as our conversation developed into some sort of casual talk.

One week after the first interview, I was informed that the manager (also my interviewer) was keen to move forward with my second application (as a Commercial Research Analyst) but wanted me to be interviewed again by his team members before making his decision. The next few days I saw myself being interviewed again by another 2 colleagues of his.

Overall I was interviewed for three rounds (or four if the first video interview is also counted). I don’t think it is bad or good, but I think it is really helpful for me to know more about the company culture, the job itself, and also the people by having multiple chances to converse with them.

The Beginning

The next morning, I was informed that I have the green light. I was verbally offered the position and earlier today, the written offer was sent to me. I was and am really happy and relieved as my hard work for the past nine months of job hunting finally paid off and come to a close.

Now, I’m looking forward to completing all the necessary documents for the EP application, and hopefully, I will start working by mid-September.

A few things to learn from this experience are:

  1. To always take the initiative to talk/chat with the HR personnel for follow-ups.
  2. Be open-minded and willing to learn when being interviewed,
  3. Stay calm under pressure and explicitly show how you process the information given to you whenever you are asked any question during an interview.

Thank you to all my friends and family who have supported and guided me throughout the process. And for everyone else, I hope you can learn something from this experience and I wish you success in your interview!
